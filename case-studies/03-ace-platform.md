# Replacing Five Systems with One Device for 5,000 Store Colleagues

Five years as sole designer on an award-winning enterprise platform deployed across 450+ stores.

---

**What:** ACE: an enterprise Android platform replacing fragmented legacy systems with a single handheld device for every store colleague
**My role:** Sole UX designer. End-to-end: navigation architecture, information hierarchy, design system, 15+ features
**Timeline:** 5 years and ongoing
**Outcome:** 450+ stores, 5,000+ daily users, 7,000 devices deployed in 5 weeks. Winner, Technology Implementation of the Year, 2022 Retail Technology Innovation Awards

## A fragmented estate that worked against its users

Before ACE, store colleagues were working across a patchwork of disconnected tools. A shared iPad ran outdated software that had been built without UX input or colleague feedback, and it was counter-intuitive enough that basic tasks took too many steps. Stock management and ticket printing lived on a separate device. Anything more complex meant walking to the back of the store to use a desktop PC. There was no way for colleagues to communicate with each other on the shop floor.

The goal was to unify everything into a single handheld device, but this wasn't a reskin or a migration of existing screens. We needed to replatform dozens of different tasks, each with their own workflows and edge cases, onto Android hardware that was locked down for enterprise use. Native Android navigation was completely disabled, which meant our interface had to handle everything: app switching, back navigation, home access, and notifications.

## Spending time where the work happens

I worked directly with store colleagues throughout the project, not through surveys or secondhand feedback but by actually spending time in stores watching how people worked. Which tasks take longest? Where do they get stuck? What information do they need and when do they need it?

This shaped everything from the information architecture to the default behaviours. The barcode scanner becoming the primary input method came directly from observing how colleagues already interacted with products and customers on the shop floor. Scan a product and you go straight to its inventory information. Scan a customer's Pets Club card and you go straight to their account. The device anticipates the task based on what's been scanned, which means colleagues spend less time navigating menus and more time doing the thing they were actually trying to do.

## The constraint that shaped everything

With native Android buttons disabled, I had to design a complete navigation model from scratch. It needed to handle app switching, back navigation, home access, and notifications across 15+ different features, and it needed to be learnable in minutes by colleagues who described themselves as "not good with technology."

[IMAGE: Navigation system — screens showing the custom nav, home screen, favourites customisation]

The favourites system came from following established patterns rather than inventing new ones. Colleagues already knew how their personal Android phones worked, so letting them pin the tools they use most to a customisable homescreen meant less learning and faster access to the things they actually need. Different roles get different setups naturally, without requiring configuration by managers. A colleague focused on adoptions sees different tools than one focused on stock replenishment, and that customisation happens through use rather than through an admin panel.

The design system behind ACE had to be versatile enough to support rapid development across genuinely different feature types: inventory management, customer accounts, subscriptions, adoption centre processes, click and collect, voice communications. Building something that maintained consistency across 15+ features designed over five years while still being flexible enough for new capabilities is a different kind of design challenge to a single product redesign. It forces you to think in patterns and principles rather than individual screens.

## Scale and sustained adoption

The migration strategy mattered as much as the design itself. We moved features onto ACE progressively, working with pilot stores to prove each new capability was better than what it replaced before rolling it out more widely. Once the critical mass of features had moved across, the legacy devices could be retired entirely. 7,000 devices were deployed across all 450+ stores in five weeks.

Over 5,000 colleagues use ACE daily. Training requirements have stayed minimal throughout, which is the strongest signal I have that the design is doing its job. The business continues to invest in new features, and colleague feedback stays positive because I keep communication channels open through senior store manager discussions and retail operations conversations. The project won Technology Implementation of the Year at the 2022 Retail Technology Innovation Awards.

## Five years of learning

Five years as sole designer on a single platform is unusual, and it teaches you things that shorter projects can't. You see your early decisions play out at scale and you learn which ones held up and which ones you'd change. Some of my early navigation decisions were solid foundations that still work. Others I've iterated on as the feature set grew and the original assumptions were tested by new use cases.

The most important lesson was that the migration strategy mattered as much as the design. Getting colleagues to actually adopt a new system isn't a design problem in isolation. It's about moving them progressively, proving each new capability is better than what it replaced, and keeping communication channels open so problems surface early rather than festering in stores where nobody from the product team is listening.

The current project is migrating a desktop-based compliance system with dozens of eForms onto the ACE device, simplifying it into a mobile-first, task-focused experience that proactively tells colleagues what they need to do and when. That work is ongoing and it draws on everything I've learned about how colleagues actually use the device in practice.
